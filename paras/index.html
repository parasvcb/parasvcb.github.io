About

I am a computational biologist who is enthusiastic about using both traditional and modern computational tools to advance understanding of biological systems. My doctoral training has been interdisciplinary, spanning computational structural biology and evolutionary genomics. I have been fortunate to contribute to research ranging from single protein studies to organism-wide proteome diversification.

       My research started with protein sequence and its inter-relationship with three-dimensional structure and function, where I used modeling techniques and molecular dynamics simulations. Along the way, I have worked on identifying super-ring H-bond interaction motifs in spider silk, highlighting evolutionary creativity shaping its molecular attributes. However, under the initiative of functional genomics, my research advanced to detail how organisms diversify proteomes through a limited gene pool. In this context, recently, I was grateful to be part of a team developing an ‚Äúexon grammar‚Äù approach, which aims to annotate exons in a way that could help us better understand how their inclusion affects protein diversity. To help the community assess the intra-gene proteome diversity, I developed a user-friendly database to visualize these annotations. I see this as an early step toward translating multi-omics data into practical insights. This will bridge the gap between two well-researched fields, post-transcriptional regulation, and corresponding protein level reorganization, which is essential to understanding physiological adaptations and cancerous states.

       I have collaborated with experimental groups at IISER-Mohali and helped them interpret the impact of long-ranging mutations and annotate newly assembled genome. As a data enthusiast, I'm always looking to apply my skills to new projects, focusing on contributing meaningful research.

Research Experience

Exon nomenclature and classification of transcripts (ENACT) framework and database development

2019 - 2023      Publication Database      Publication Method Advisor: Dr. Shashi B. Pandit, Associate Professor (Biology), IISER-M
ReactJS Django MySQL Apache Transcriptomics Genomic annotation BioPython RefSeq data extraction Pfam PsiPred

Eukaryotic genomes compensate for disparities between gene count and expressed protein by several mechanisms, where the contribution of alternate splicing (AS) is commendable. To understand how splicing impacts gene products, a distinction between alternate transcription (ATR) and alternate translation (ATL) and focus on AS is necessary. I developed the ENACT framework to address this need, which systematically annotates and classifies exons from a gene perspective.

Key Achievements:

  • ENACT annotations were performed on protein-coding genes of nematodes, flies, fish, mice, and humans, with the results presented in the Nightingale library views available as ENACTdb at https://www.iscbglab.in/enactdb.
  • Identified diverse exon features, including variable prevalence in transcripts, differential translational characteristics, and splice site preferences, with a noteworthy trade-off between exon skipping events and alternate splice site choices across organisms.
  • A significant abundance of alternate exons in untranslated regions (UTRs) and intervening regions between UTRs and coding sequences (CDS) was noticed in mammals, underscoring their regulatory roles.
  • The synergistic interplay between ATR and AS processes shapes the diversity of the human proteome, where AS-impacted region exhibits a higher fraction of domains per gene with this fraction remaining relatively stable across isoforms.

Understanding the sequence optimization of spider silk nanocrystals by comparative evaluation of native with globular protein-inspired geometries

2021      Publication Advisor: Dr. Shashi B. Pandit & Dr. Kamal P. Singh, IISER-M
HPC Steered MD NAMD VMD and Tcl Scripting MDTraj BioPython Interfacial packing analysis Dynamic tracking of interaction motif

Spider silk is a remarkable material known for its exceptional strength and toughness, mainly due to its Œ≤-sheet nanocrystals interspersed within intrinsically disordered regions which are basis of its strength and flexibility. Our computational study aimed to optimize the sequence of these nanocrystals to enhance their physical resilience.

Key Achievements:

  • We compared naturally occurring silk nanocrystals with in-silico models inspired by globular proteins that favor similar structures.
  • Using the Steered Molecular Dynamics (SMD) protocol, we subjected these models to fracture simulations with constant velocity pulls to understand the molecular basis of their strength.
  • Our results demonstrated that natural silk exhibits a geometrical sensitivity in interstrand hydrogen bonding, which maintains cooperativity. This cooperativity is lost in the continuum of in silico models, indicating the superiority of the natural sequence in terms of packing and cooperativity. We consider them as important tertary interaction motif.
  • To ensure the robustness of our findings, simulations were conducted at two different pulling speeds, and the results remained consistent, accounting for noise propagation.

I also contributed to rationalizing the construction of generic models involving intrinsically disordered regions (IDR) and nanocrystalline regimes. This was to simulate other experimental observations from our collaborators, which, while not part of the manuscript, were crucial for the broader application of our findings.

Understanding the Molecular Basis of Compromise in the Pore-Forming Activity of Vibrio cholerae Cytolysin (VCC) upon Mutation of Residue Y321 to Alanine

2020      Publication Advisor: Dr. Shashi B. Pandit & Dr. Kausik Chattopadhyay, IISER-M
HPC Molecular Dynamics PCA (Bio3D) VMD and Tcl Scripting MDTraj Network centrality Correlation network analysis Community partitioning

Vibrio cholerae cytolysin (VCC) is a potent, membrane-damaging secretory toxin that assembles into a heptameric pore to cause severe diarrheal diseases. This transformation requires significant conformational changes from its monomeric form. Our study investigates the impact of a specific mutation, tyrosine (Y) to alanine (A) at residue 321 (Y321A), on the toxin's functionality.

Key Achievements:

  • Mutation Y321A significantly hampers VCC's assembly and pore-forming activity.
  • We utilized principal component analysis (PCA) to model and compare the global conformational changes in VCC's wild-type and Y321A mutant forms. Correlation network analysis (CNA) further elucidated the structural and functional disruptions caused by the mutation.
  • The Y321A mutation disrupts a crucial hydrophobic pocket, leading to failed compensation in interdomain contacts among the Œ≤-Trefoil, Œ≤-Prism, and conserved cytolysin domains. This disruption is critical to the observed functional arrest and impeded domain motion necessary for transformation to active form.

To add computational rigor, I performed difference contact network analysis to elucidate further the structural changes (not part of manuscript). I simulated the partially recovering Y321F variant, which showed recovery in network structure. Moreover, additional hub residues implicated in the structural integrity are being experimentally validated, showing promising preliminary results.
